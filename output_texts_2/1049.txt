It‚Äôs now more than 10 years since a Firefox plugin called Firesheep hit the news ‚Äì if you were interested in cybersecurity back in 2010, you will almost certainly remember that name.

Back then, many websites where security and privacy were important ‚Äì examples include social networks, car rental firms, online support forums and even banks ‚Äì paid only lip service to HTTPS.
They would use encrypted connections when it would very clearly have been dangerous to do otherwise, such as on the login page where you entered your actual password, or on the payment page where you put in your credit card details.
But they would drop back to HTTP for everything else because it was a bit faster and easier ‚Äì you didn‚Äôt need to spend extra time and CPU power at each end encrypting and decrypting every data packet that you sent and received.
What Firesheep did was to turn the Firefox browser into an easy-to-use network sniffer ‚Äì that‚Äôs the jargon term for a network surveillance tool ‚Äì that just about anyone could use, regardless of their technical skill.
Firesheep would automatically sniff out other people‚Äôs social networking connections, wait until after the secure login part that couldn‚Äôt be eavesdropped because of HTTPS encryption, and then target the insecure HTTP traffic that followed.
Firesheep would read in the headers from those unencrypted HTTP web requests, extract the session cookies or authentication tokens that denoted the user‚Äôs identity, use the stolen authentication data to impersonate the unfortunate user, and hijack their account.
All of this was done automatically, right inside a browser where an attacker could point-and-click to exploit any hacked accounts at once.
In theory, anyone in the coffee shop around you could have been running Firesheep, digging around in your Facebook account or posting on your Twitter feed, and you wouldn‚Äôt have realised until it was too late.
Firesheep certainly put the cryptographic cat amongst the pigeons, if you will pardon the mixed faunal metaphor.
Indeed, the Firesheep story was an important catalyst in persuading most of the major players in search, social media and online services to bite the bullet and use HTTPS all the time.
Facebook, for example, made ‚ÄúHTTPS always‚Äù an option in 2011, turned it on for everyone in North America in 2012, and by 2013 had pretty much abandoned HTTP altogether.
Apple‚Äôs App Store moved over to HTTPS in 2013; Microsoft pledged to encrypt almost everything back in 2013; and Google made Gmail HTTPS-only in 2014. 
By 2015, Google‚Äôs search ranking algorithms were downvoting sites that didn‚Äôt offer HTTPS versions; in 2017, the search giant started publicly shaming login and credit card pages that still used HTTP by labelling them ‚Äúnot secure‚Äú; and by 2018, Google was applying that label to any website that hadn‚Äôt upgraded to HTTPS.
HTTP, in other words, has been inexorably waning for the past decade.
Well, it‚Äôs 2021, and the vast majority of websites now support HTTPS.
Think of how long it‚Äôs been since you found a mainstream website ‚Äì indeed, any website ‚Äì that didn‚Äôt offer HTTPS if you insisted‚Ä¶

More importantly, why is HTTP still the default choice of your browser if you type an URL into the address bar and don‚Äôt explicitly put https:// at the start?
Why don‚Äôt browsers these days assume we mean HTTPS unless we go out of our way to type in http:// instead?
The simple answer is that there are still enough non-HTTPS websites left out there that switching to HTTPS by default would cause sufficiently many transitory technical hassles to be disruptive.
Sadly, inadvertent disruptions caused by well-meaning efforts to improve cybersecurity often have the undesirable and paradoxical consequence of luring less well-informed users into deliberately reducing the security they already have, as a ‚Äúworkaround‚Äù to bypass the ‚Äúproblem‚Äù.
